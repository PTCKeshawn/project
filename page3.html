There's a SECOND Industrial Revolution?! : The Industrial Shift

Yes! In the late 19th and 20th centuries, a “new” Industrial Revolution emerged, building on the old but introducing major changes. Industries began using new materials like lighter metals, rare earths, plastics, and synthetic products, along with new energy sources. This period also saw the rise of advanced machines, tools, and computers that led to the development of fully automatic factories, going beyond the earlier mechanization and assembly lines. Ownership of production shifted from a few wealthy individuals to a broader base through stock purchases by individuals and institutions. Many European countries began socializing key parts of their economies, and governments took a more active role in managing industrial societies, moving away from the hands-off “laissez-faire” approach. However, starting in the 1980s, countries like the U.S. and the U.K. reversed this trend, reducing government involvement in the economy.

Inventors and what they Invented:

  • Thomas Edison:
    invented the electric light bulb, which provided safe and reliable lighting, allowing people to work and live more comfortably after dark.

  • Nikola Tesla:
    developed the alternating current (AC) electricity system, which allowed electricity to be transmitted over long distances and powered homes, factories, and cities.

  • Alexander Graham Bell:
    invented the telephone, making it possible for people to communicate instantly across long distances, which revolutionized personal and business communication.

  • J.P. Morgan:
    he financed and organized major industries like steel, electricity, and railroads, helping to grow the U.S. economy and support inventors like Thomas Edison.
